Teaching Kit

User Research Teaching Kit

How can we explain the importance of an Internet that treats content providers, users and information equally? These activities help learners analyze, create and take action around Net Neutrality in a variety of settings, from Maker Parties to classrooms.

Made by Kat Braybrooke and Chad Sansing, with thanks to Laura Hilliger, Dave Steer, Alina Hua, Jess Klein and Karen Smith.


Introduction

The Internet is great because it belongs to all of us equally, providing a level playing field that has fuelled innovation while enabling free expression. The web as we know it today is founded on the principle of 'Net Neutrality' -- the idea that data, no matter who it is coming from or who is receiving it, should be given the same priority by Internet Service Providers.

Policymakers in the US are currently considering whether to adopt rules to protect Net Neutrality -- or to undermine it by developing corporate 'fast lanes'. We are worried that such policies would eliminate the level playing field that makes the open web so innovative and resilient.

The activities below have been built to help learners of all ages discover, create and take action around Net Neutrality. They are organized into 3 categories of activities that you can mix, match and remix. For example, Ankjit in India could choose a quick activity from BACKGROUND and one from TAKE ACTION for his kitchen table workshop with family in Kerala.

Agenda

NET NEUTRALITY // BACKGROUND
Explain the fundamentals of how the web fits together

  1. Catch a network signal! (Author: Team Mesh)
    Explore how network connectivity works through play. (30 minutes)
  2. Web mechanics speed dating (Author: Julia Vallera, Hive NYC)
    Illustrate the ways different web elements are related. (30 minutes)
  3. A strong wind blows for the open web (Author: Laura Hilliger)
    Remix this icebreaker to introduce Net Neutrality concepts. (15 minutes)
  4. Net Neutrality Bingo (Author: Karen Smith)
    Explore beginning Net Neutrality concepts with a group of learners. (1 hour)

NET NEUTRALITY // DISCOVER
Teach the value of Net Neutrality in everyday life

  1. Videojam: Life in the Slowww Lane (Author: Kat Braybrooke)
    Get creative by remixing "chopped and screwed" versions of a throttled web. (1-2 hours)
  2. Superheroes for Net Neutrality (Author: Jess Klein)
    Craft a league of superheroes to save #TeamInternet. (2 hours)
  3. Trace the outline of Net Neutrality at home (Author: Chad Sansing)
    Who owns my fiber, and what can they do with it if we lose Net Neutrality? (2 hours)
  4. Explain Net Neutrality to _____ (Author: Kat Braybrooke)
    A lesson in creative media framing through Popcorn video remix. (1 hour)

NET NEUTRALITY // TAKE ACTION
Empower learners to provoke change in their communities

  1. Design a "Support Net Neutrality" poster (Author: Chad Sansing)
    Build awareness while getting creative. (1-2 hours)
  2. I Fight for Net Neutrality Because... (Author: Jess Klein)
    Start a meme with learners by creating your own version of this fun make. (30 min)
  3. Pressure the FCC to take action (Author: Various partners)
    Try creating your own activity with this Mozilla petition in the US and this Open Media petition in Canada.
  4. How to take action on Net Neutrality in [Your Country] (Author: YOU!)
    In-progress activities added by local communities. Get in touch to add!

Learning assessment

By participating in these activities, learners should be able to voice their own opinions on Net Neutrality, and take action to hold leaders accountable. Learners should also be able to understand the relevance of Net Neutrality for everyone who uses the Internet, framed around these concepts:

  • Net Neutrality helps ensure equity of access online.
  • The loss of Net Neutrality compromises the Web in fundamental ways.
  • The loss of Net Neutrality will result in an Internet different from the one we have today.
  • To protect Net Neutrality, ISPs should be prohibited from unduly blocking, prioritizing, or discriminating content, and these policies should be applied online and in mobile environments.
  • Net Neutrality is a people issue, and it's an equality issue; caring about it is caring about your fellow people.
